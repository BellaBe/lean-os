# Behavioral Anti-Patterns

What to avoid when applying behavioral science. These mistakes undermine effectiveness or damage trust.

---

## Anti-Pattern 1: Pattern Stacking

### What it looks like
Landing page with 10+ biases crammed into every element:
- Headline: Loss frame + curiosity + social proof
- Subhead: Scarcity + authority + more social proof
- Every paragraph: Another bias layered on
- CTA: Urgency + loss + commitment + friction reduction all at once

### Why it fails
- Feels manipulative and desperate
- Dilutes each pattern's impact
- Creates cognitive overload
- Sophisticated buyers recognize and distrust
- Violates principle: persuasion should feel helpful, not pushy

### Instead
Select 2-4 primary biases based on the key barrier to action:
- If problem is awareness → Curiosity, Distinctiveness
- If problem is trust → Social Proof, Authority
- If problem is urgency → Scarcity, Loss Aversion
- If problem is friction → Simplification, Defaults

### Detection
Count biases per content piece. Flag if >5 distinct biases.

## Anti-Pattern 2: Fake Scarcity

### What it looks like
- Countdown timer that resets on page refresh
- "Only 3 left!" that never changes
- "Limited time offer" that runs forever
- Artificial deadlines with no real constraint
- "Spots filling fast" when capacity is unlimited

### Why it fails
- **Trust destruction is permanent**: Once discovered, buyer never trusts again
- **Word spreads**: Reviews, social media, communities share these discoveries
- **Sophisticated buyers test**: They refresh the page, check archives, compare over time
- **Legal risk**: FTC considers fake scarcity deceptive trade practice

### Instead
Create legitimate scarcity:
- Implementation capacity limits (real team bandwidth)
- Genuine price increase dates (published in advance)
- Cohort-based enrollment (real start dates)
- Beta/early access windows (real program phases)
- Event capacity (real venue/bandwidth limits)

If no real scarcity exists, don't manufacture it. Use other biases.

### Detection
Ask: "If a journalist investigated, would this hold up?" If no, it's fake.

## Anti-Pattern 3: Loss Frame Overload

### What it looks like
Every piece of content framed as loss:
- "Stop losing money"
- "Don't miss out"
- "You're falling behind"
- "Your competitors are winning"
- "Every day you wait costs you"

### Why it fails
- Creates anxiety instead of motivation
- Becomes noise (habituation)
- Feels threatening rather than helpful
- Can trigger defensive rejection
- Damages brand perception (doom-and-gloom association)

### Instead
Balance loss and gain framing:
- **Headlines/CTAs**: Loss framing works (2x impact on action)
- **Body content**: Mix of loss (problem) and gain (solution)
- **Overall tone**: Solution-focused, not fear-focused
- **Brand consideration**: Some brands shouldn't use heavy loss framing

Rule of thumb: 60% gain / 40% loss for body content, loss for headlines/CTAs only.

### Detection
Count loss-framed sentences vs gain-framed. Flag if >70% loss.

## Anti-Pattern 4: Generic Social Proof

### What it looks like
- "Trusted by thousands"
- "Industry leader"
- "Millions of satisfied customers"
- "Used worldwide"
- "Award-winning" (no specific award named)

### Why it fails
- Vague claims are ignored (processed as marketing fluff)
- No mental anchor (can't picture "thousands")
- Not relatable (not "people like me")
- Unprovable = unbelievable
- Competitors say the same thing

### Instead
Make proof specific and relevant:
- **Specific numbers**: "47,382 teams" not "thousands"
- **Peer relevance**: "Used by 73% of YC companies" not "used worldwide"
- **Named proof**: "Trusted by Stripe, Notion, Linear" not "industry leaders"
- **Implied proof**: Activity feeds, user counts, real-time signups
- **Contextual**: Match proof to reader's situation

Hierarchy: Implied > Specific peer > Named > Numbered > Vague

### Detection
Does the social proof pass the "so what?" test? Can reader picture it? Does it match their context?

## Anti-Pattern 5: Friction Neglect

### What it looks like
- Great copy, but 12-field form
- Compelling offer, but 5-step checkout
- Strong CTA, but 3 competing buttons
- Persuasive email, but link goes to homepage not landing page
- Optimized landing page, but requires account creation to see pricing

### Why it fails
- Each friction point loses 5-10% of visitors
- No amount of persuasion overcomes bad UX
- Friction compounds (5 steps = 23-41% of original traffic)
- Mobile friction is worse (smaller targets, more effort)
- Biases amplify existing motivation, they can't create it from zero

### Instead
Audit and remove friction before applying biases:
1. Map the path from first touch to conversion
2. Count clicks, fields, decisions, page loads
3. Remove everything non-essential
4. Add friction reducers (social login, autofill, progress bars)
5. Then apply biases to the streamlined experience

Friction reduction often beats bias application for conversion lift.

### Detection
Walk the conversion path yourself. Count: clicks, fields, pages, decisions. Minimum viable?

## Anti-Pattern 6: Commitment Coercion

### What it looks like
- Asking for meeting on first email (no relationship)
- "You said X, so you have to do Y" (aggressive callback)
- Forcing verbal commitments that feel manipulative
- Using past behavior against someone

### Why it fails
- Commitment works through self-perception, not coercion
- Forced commitments create resentment, not consistency
- Sophisticated buyers recognize the tactic
- Damages relationship and trust
- May work once but kills lifetime value

### Instead
Build commitment ladder naturally:
1. **Micro-commitment**: Answer a question, click a link
2. **Small investment**: Download content, watch video
3. **Medium engagement**: Attend webinar, take meeting
4. **Larger commitment**: Start trial, pilot program
5. **Full commitment**: Purchase, contract

Each step should feel like natural progression, not manipulation.

### Detection
Would you feel comfortable if the commitment request was recorded and shared?

## Anti-Pattern 7: Deceptive Anchoring

### What it looks like
- Inflated "original price" that was never real
- Competitor comparison using outdated or wrong data
- ROI calculations with cherry-picked assumptions
- "Value" stacking with made-up dollar amounts
- Misleading unit comparisons (different quantities)

### Why it fails
- Discoverable deception destroys trust
- FTC/legal risk for false advertising
- Sophisticated buyers verify claims
- Short-term gain, long-term reputation damage
- Word of mouth amplifies negative discoveries

### Instead
Use honest anchoring:
- **Real alternatives**: Actual cost of hiring, real competitor prices
- **Verified comparisons**: Cite sources, use current data
- **Conservative ROI**: Use achievable results, not best-case
- **Defensible value**: Only claim value you can justify
- **Transparent methodology**: Show how you calculated

Anchoring works with honest numbers. Don't risk trust for marginally higher anchor.

### Detection
Would this anchor survive fact-checking? Can you prove it?

## Anti-Pattern 8: Ignoring Audience Sophistication

### What it looks like
- Using consumer tactics on enterprise buyers
- Obvious manipulation on technical audiences
- Heavy urgency on risk-averse prospects
- "Clickbait" on professional content
- Same playbook for all segments

### Why it fails
- Sophisticated audiences recognize and reject manipulation
- B2B buyers are trained to evaluate
- Technical audiences require evidence
- Enterprise has longer, committee-based decisions
- One-size-fits-all wastes resources and damages brand

### Instead
Calibrate bias intensity to audience:

| Audience | Approach |
|----------|----------|
| Consumer, impulse | Can use stronger biases, urgency |
| SMB | Moderate intensity, quick proof |
| Mid-market | Evidence-based, social proof heavy |
| Enterprise | Subtle, authority-focused, risk-addressed |
| Technical | Logic, proof, mechanism explanation |

### Detection
Would your target audience feel respected by this approach?

## Anti-Pattern 9: Reciprocity Violation

### What it looks like
- Demanding meeting after one "value" email
- Gated content that doesn't deliver value
- "Free consultation" that's a hard sales pitch
- Expecting response after generic outreach
- Keeping score visibly ("I gave you X, now you owe me Y")

### Why it fails
- Reciprocity requires genuine value first
- Transactional framing kills the effect
- Low-value "gifts" don't create obligation
- Immediate ask negates reciprocity
- Visible expectation feels manipulative

### Instead
Give genuinely, without immediate expectation:
1. Provide value that stands alone (useful even if they never buy)
2. Give multiple times before asking
3. Never mention what you "gave" when asking
4. Accept that some won't reciprocate (that's fine)
5. Make the ask proportional to value given

### Detection
Would you deliver this value even if you could never sell to them?

## Anti-Pattern 10: Proof-Claim Mismatch

### What it looks like
- "Fastest platform" with no benchmark
- "Best-in-class" with no comparison
- "Revolutionary" with no evidence of revolution
- Customer quotes about service paired with product claims
- Enterprise logos but SMB testimonials

### Why it fails
- Mismatched proof doesn't support claim
- Sophisticated buyers notice the gap
- Generic proof doesn't validate specific claims
- Creates doubt about all claims
- Wasted proof opportunity

### Instead
Match proof to claim precisely:

| Claim Type | Proof Required |
|------------|----------------|
| Speed/performance | Benchmarks, third-party tests |
| Results/ROI | Customer data, case studies |
| Ease of use | Demo, user testimonials about onboarding |
| Enterprise-ready | Enterprise customer logos, compliance certs |
| Innovation | Patents, unique features, expert validation |

### Detection
For each claim, ask: "What specific evidence supports this exact claim?"

## The Meta-Principle

**Behavioral science amplifies, it doesn't create.**

If your product doesn't deliver value, biases won't save it. If your claim isn't true, framing won't make it true. If your audience doesn't trust you, tactics won't rebuild trust.

Use behavioral science to:
- Help people overcome inertia to do things they want to do
- Make true claims more compelling
- Reduce friction to good decisions
- Build genuine trust faster

Not to:
- Trick people into things they'll regret
- Make false claims believable
- Manipulate people against their interests
- Manufacture trust you haven't earned
